# Progressive Overload

Progressive overload is the principle that, to keep improving, the demands placed
on the body must gradually increase over time. Once a stimulus becomes routine,
the body has already adapted to it and further change slows or stalls. Adding a
small, manageable challenge re-starts the adaptation process.

## Ways to add overload

Load is only one lever. You can progress by:

- **Load** — adding weight to the bar or machine.
- **Volume** — adding sets or reps at a given load.
- **Frequency** — training a movement or muscle group more often per week.
- **Tempo / range of motion** — slower eccentrics, fuller range, or pauses.
- **Density** — doing the same work in less time (shorter rest).

Rotating which lever you push helps avoid overloading a single joint or tissue
too aggressively.

## How fast to progress

Progression should be small and sustainable. For most trained lifters, adding a
rep or a little load when the current target feels controlled (and technique
holds) is enough. Beginners can often progress session to session; more advanced
lifters progress over weeks. Trying to add load every session indefinitely leads
to stalls and injury risk.

## Practical guidance

- Pick one or two progression levers per block rather than everything at once.
- Keep one or two reps "in reserve" on most working sets to protect technique.
- Track your sessions; you cannot progressively overload what you do not measure.
- Expect plateaus — they are a signal to change a variable, deload, or check
  recovery, not to simply add more.

Progressive overload sits underneath nearly every other training method:
periodization, autoregulation, and volume landmarks are all ways of *managing*
overload over time.

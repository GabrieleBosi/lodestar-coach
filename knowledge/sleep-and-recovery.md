# Sleep and Recovery

Sleep is one of the most powerful and underrated recovery tools. It is when much
of the body's repair, hormonal regulation, and memory consolidation (including
motor learning of new movements) takes place. Chronic short sleep is associated
with worse performance, impaired recovery, increased injury risk, greater
perceived effort, and disrupted appetite regulation.

## How much

Most adults are best served by roughly **7–9 hours per night**. Hard-training
individuals often sit toward the upper end of that range, and some benefit from
additional sleep during heavy training blocks.

## Consequences of poor sleep

- Reduced strength and endurance output, and slower skill acquisition.
- Higher perceived exertion, so the same training feels harder.
- Impaired glucose handling and appetite signals, complicating nutrition goals.
- Blunted recovery between sessions.

## Sleep hygiene basics

- **Consistency:** keep similar sleep and wake times, including weekends.
- **Light:** get daylight in the morning; dim lights and reduce screens before bed.
- **Environment:** a cool, dark, quiet room supports deeper sleep.
- **Stimulants:** limit caffeine in the second half of the day; it has a long
  half-life.
- **Wind-down:** a calm pre-bed routine helps the transition to sleep.

## Practical guidance

- Treat sleep as part of the training plan, not an afterthought.
- If sleep is short, protecting it usually beats adding more training volume.
- Naps (short, early-afternoon) can supplement, but do not fully replace, nightly
  sleep.

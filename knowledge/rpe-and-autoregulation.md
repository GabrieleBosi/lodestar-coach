# RPE and Autoregulation

Autoregulation means adjusting training in real time to how you actually feel and
perform on a given day, rather than following a fixed number rigidly. The most
common tool for this in strength training is a **Rating of Perceived Exertion
(RPE)** scale based on **reps in reserve (RIR)**.

## The RIR-based RPE scale

On the common 1–10 lifting scale:

- **RPE 10** — maximal; no more reps possible (0 reps in reserve).
- **RPE 9** — 1 rep in reserve.
- **RPE 8** — 2 reps in reserve.
- **RPE 7** — 3 reps in reserve.

Most hypertrophy and strength work is productive in roughly the RPE 7–9 range,
leaving one to three reps in reserve on working sets.

## Why autoregulate

Readiness fluctuates with sleep, stress, nutrition, and life load. A load that is
an RPE 8 on a good day might be an RPE 9–10 on a poor one. Autoregulation lets you
hit the intended *effort* even when the right *load* changes day to day, keeping
the stimulus appropriate and reducing wasted or risky sessions.

## How to apply it

- Assign target RPE/RIR ranges to sets, not just fixed weights.
- If the prescribed load exceeds the target RPE, reduce it; if it is easier, you
  may add a little.
- Use RPE to decide when to stop adding sets (e.g., stop when bar speed drops or
  RPE climbs faster than planned).
- Beginners often misjudge proximity to failure; RPE accuracy improves with
  experience, so pair it with simple performance tracking early on.

Autoregulation complements periodization: the plan sets the direction, RPE tunes
the daily dose.

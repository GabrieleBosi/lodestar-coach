# Protein Intake for Training

Dietary protein supplies the amino acids used to repair and build muscle in
response to training. For people who train regularly, protein needs are higher
than the basic sedentary requirement.

## How much

For active individuals aiming to build or maintain muscle, a commonly recommended
intake is roughly **1.6 g of protein per kg of body weight per day**, with
benefits typically plateauing somewhere around **1.6–2.2 g/kg/day**. During a
calorie deficit (fat loss), the higher end of that range helps preserve lean mass.

These are general ranges for healthy people; individual needs vary, and this is
not medical advice.

## Distribution

Spreading protein across the day — roughly **3–4 meals each containing on the
order of 0.3–0.4 g/kg** — tends to support muscle protein synthesis better than
getting most protein in a single meal. A protein-containing meal within a few
hours around training is sensible, though total daily intake matters most.

## Quality and sources

Protein "quality" reflects its amino acid profile and digestibility, especially
the leucine content that helps trigger muscle protein synthesis. Animal sources
(dairy, eggs, meat, fish) are complete and leucine-rich. Plant-based eaters can
meet needs well by eating a variety of sources (legumes, soy, grains) and
slightly higher total intake to cover lower per-gram quality.

## Practical guidance

- Estimate a daily target from body weight, then divide across meals.
- Anchor each main meal with a clear protein source.
- Prioritise hitting the daily total before worrying about precise timing.

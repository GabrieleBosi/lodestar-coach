# Hydration for Training

Water supports temperature regulation, joint lubrication, nutrient transport, and
cardiovascular function. Meaningful dehydration can impair both endurance and,
to a lesser degree, strength performance, as well as perceived effort and
concentration.

## How much

Daily fluid needs vary widely with body size, climate, and sweat rate, so fixed
numbers are only rough guides. A practical, low-tech indicator is urine colour:
pale-yellow generally suggests adequate hydration, while consistently dark urine
suggests you need more fluid.

## Around training

- **Before:** arrive well hydrated; drink normally in the hours beforehand.
- **During:** for sessions under ~60 minutes, water as thirst dictates is usually
  enough. For longer or very sweaty sessions, drinking to limit large body-mass
  losses helps.
- **After:** replace fluid lost as sweat over the following hours; weighing
  before and after long sessions can estimate losses.

## Electrolytes

Sweat contains sodium and other electrolytes. For most short sessions, normal
meals replace these. During prolonged exercise, heavy sweating, or hot
conditions, including sodium (in a sports drink or food) supports fluid retention
and reduces the risk of low blood sodium from drinking large volumes of plain
water.

## Practical guidance

- Use thirst and urine colour as everyday guides.
- Scale fluid (and sodium) up for long, hot, or very sweaty sessions.
- Avoid both chronic under-drinking and force-drinking far beyond thirst.

General information only — not medical advice, especially for those with medical
conditions affecting fluid or sodium balance.

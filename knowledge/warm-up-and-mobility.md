# Warm-up and Mobility

A warm-up raises tissue temperature, increases blood flow, and rehearses the
movements you are about to train. The goal is to arrive at your working sets
feeling prepared and moving well — not to fatigue yourself before the session.

## A simple, general structure

1. **General warm-up (3–5 min):** light cardio (bike, row, brisk walk) to raise
   heart rate and temperature.
2. **Dynamic mobility:** controlled movements through the ranges you will use
   (e.g., leg swings, hip openers, shoulder circles). Favour active, dynamic
   drills over long static holds before lifting.
3. **Specific warm-up sets:** ramp up to your working load with progressively
   heavier sets of the actual exercise (e.g., a few sets building to your first
   work set).

## Static vs. dynamic stretching

Brief static stretching is fine, but long static holds immediately before heavy
or explosive efforts may transiently reduce force output. Dynamic stretching and
ramp-up sets are generally preferred right before training; static stretching for
flexibility goals is better placed after training or in separate sessions.

## Mobility work

"Mobility" is usable range of motion under control. If a position you need (deep
squat, overhead reach) is limited, targeted drills for that joint, done
consistently, tend to help more than generic stretching. Train the ranges you
actually want to own.

## Practical guidance

- Keep warm-ups specific to the day's session; a heavy squat day needs different
  prep than an upper-body day.
- Warm up enough to move well, but stop short of accumulating fatigue.
- Address recurring tight or painful positions with consistent, targeted work
  rather than one-off stretching.

# Energy Balance

Energy balance is the relationship between the calories you consume and the
calories you expend. Over time it is the primary determinant of body-weight
change.

- **Surplus** (intake > expenditure): body mass tends to increase.
- **Deficit** (intake < expenditure): body mass tends to decrease.
- **Maintenance** (intake ≈ expenditure): weight is roughly stable.

## Components of expenditure

Total daily energy expenditure has several parts:

- **Basal metabolic rate (BMR):** energy used at rest — usually the largest share.
- **Thermic effect of food:** energy used digesting and processing food.
- **Exercise activity:** intentional training.
- **Non-exercise activity thermogenesis (NEAT):** walking, fidgeting, daily
  movement — which can vary a lot between people and adapt to dieting.

## Setting a target

For fat loss, a **moderate deficit** (commonly around 10–25% below maintenance) is
sustainable and helps preserve muscle when paired with adequate protein and
resistance training. For muscle gain, a **small surplus** limits unnecessary fat
gain. Aggressive deficits or surpluses tend to backfire through muscle loss,
fatigue, or excess fat gain.

## Practical guidance

- Estimate maintenance from body weight and activity, then adjust based on real
  weekly trends rather than a formula's prediction.
- Track average weight over weeks; day-to-day fluctuations are mostly water and
  food in transit.
- Protein and resistance training protect lean mass during a deficit.
- Energy needs change as body weight and activity change — revisit targets
  periodically.

This is general educational information, not individualised dietary or medical
advice.
